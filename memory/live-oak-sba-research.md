## 2. WHAT LIVE OAK LOOKS FOR — THE FULL CRITERIA

### Credit Score (FICO)
- **Stated minimum:** 650
- **Preferred threshold:** 680+ (confirmed by multiple sources including Merchant Maverick, Live Oak's own blog)
- **JB's situation:** 698 — technically above minimum, below ideal. Live Oak's director Lisa Forrest has confirmed they use 650 as a floor but lenders have discretion. A 698 with a strong business story is approvable. A 698 with a weak business story gets declined.
- **Key action:** JB needs to frame this proactively: "I'm at 698 currently and actively reducing utilization — projecting 750 by June. Here's what's driving the score and why it doesn't reflect credit risk."

### DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)
- **Live Oak's internal standard:** **1.5x** — significantly higher than the SBA floor of 1.25x and most competitors (1.1–1.25x)
- **How they calculate it:** Adjusted cash flow (EBITDA minus owner salary, unfunded CapEx, taxes) divided by annual debt service
- **GFS projected DSCR:**
  - Scenario A (Standby seller note, only SBA payments counted): ~**2.97x**
  - Scenario A (Full debt service including seller note): ~**2.32x**
  - Both blow past Live Oak's 1.5x requirement with room to spare
- **This is JB's single strongest data point.** GFS more than doubles Live Oak's DSCR minimum.
- **Note from Lisa Forrest (Live Oak Director):** "DSCR is an approval standard, not a covenant." Meaning they don't require you to maintain 1.5x post-close — just to demonstrate the business can cover debt at origination. GFS has no problem here.

### Down Payment / Equity Injection
- **Minimum:** 10% of total project costs
- **For $10M deal:** That's $1M minimum
- **Seller note rule:** A seller note on full standby can count for up to 50% of the required equity injection (i.e., max $500K of the $1M minimum). The other 50% must be buyer's own unborrowed cash.
- **Goodwill caveat:** If goodwill exceeds 50% of purchase price, equity injection requirement can increase. For GFS ($7.5M business, likely significant goodwill vs. hard assets), JB should be prepared for a possible ask of 15% ($1.5M).
- **JB's structure:** $250K buyer injection + $1.15M seller note on standby = ~$1.4M total = 14% of $10M. This should work. The investor group covering additional equity also helps.

### Industry Experience
- **Live Oak's position:** They list "credit history & business experience" as a core qualification. They do NOT require exact industry match.
- **What they actually care about:** Management competence + proof you can run a service business at scale + risk that key people leave
- **The mitigant that matters most:** Retaining existing management is the #1 substitute for industry experience in SBA lending. JB has this:
  - Woody rolling 18% equity + staying involved
  - Mark Bryan (President, Sales) staying
  - Danny Martinez (CFO) staying
- **JB's operator background (Payix → $115M REPAY exit)** is the lead story. Tech-enabled service business at scale — it's not fire protection, but it's running a complex B2B services organization.
- **Confidence level:** Medium-high that Live Oak approves with no fire industry experience given strong management retention and business metrics.

### Loan Size / Deal Structure
- **Standard SBA 7(a) max:** $5M
- **Combination financing (Live Oak specialty):** SBA 7(a) at $5M + conventional loan = up to $9M total. Live Oak explicitly markets this for deals with enterprise values of $7M–$10M.
- **For JB's $10M deal:** The SBA portion covers $5M; the conventional/504 portion covers the rest. This is Live Oak's standard playbook for deals like GFS.
- **SBA 7(a) limit increase to $10M:** A bill (H.R. 3174) passed the House in December 2025 to raise the limit to $10M for manufacturing/industrial services. As of March 2026, still awaiting Senate. Do NOT count on this. Structure the deal around $5M SBA cap.
- **Terms:** Up to 10 years for business acquisition; up to 25 years if real estate is included. GFS has real estate ($2.5M), so the RE piece can go 25 years = lower monthly payment = better DSCR.

## 4. COMMON REASONS LIVE OAK DECLINES ACQUISITION LOANS

In priority order of how often they occur:

1. **Insufficient DSCR** — Business cash flow doesn't cover debt service at 1.5x. GFS is not at risk here.
2. **Low credit score** — Below 650. JB is at 698. Not a decline risk, but could be a friction point.
3. **Purchase price not supported by valuation** — SBA requires an independent business valuation. If the $10M ask doesn't pencil vs. EBITDA multiples, they may not finance the full amount. At $10M purchase / $2.94M EBITDA = 3.4x EBITDA — this is a reasonable multiple for a service business with strong growth.
4. **Inadequate equity injection** — Not enough buyer skin in the game.
5. **Weak or missing management team** — Buyer with no experience AND no retention of key employees. JB has the retention piece covered.
6. **Financial documentation issues** — Prepared statements vs. reviewed/audited. Live Oak will want tax returns to verify. JB should have GFS's 2023, 2024, and 2025 tax returns ready.
7. **Overvalued goodwill** — If business is mostly goodwill vs. hard assets, Live Oak scrutinizes harder. Fire protection has trucks, equipment, tools — that's real collateral. But most of a $10M deal will be goodwill. Worth discussing.
8. **Unresolved issues on buyer** — Judgments, liens, tax delinquencies. JB should verify his credit report has no surprises before the call.
9. **Credit card payable of $898K on GFS balance sheet** — This needs a clear explanation. Is it corporate Amex paid monthly? Revolving balance? If revolving, it's treated as working capital need. JB needs to know the answer before the call.
