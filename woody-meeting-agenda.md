# Woody Meeting — Minute-by-Minute Agenda
*GFS Fire Pros Acquisition | Jarmon Services, Inc.*
*Prepared by Ace | 2026-03-26*

## MEETING GOAL
One thing: **verbal yes/no on the deal structure**.

Closing question: *"Does this structure make sense to you? Is this something you'd want to keep exploring?"*

You are NOT closing the deal today. You are getting permission to spend money on due diligence.

---

## THE AGENDA

### MINUTES 0–5: Arrive + Set the Room

Walk in like you belong there. Be early. Greet Woody by name. Shake hands.

**If Diane is present:** Acknowledge her. She's an emotional anchor in this deal. Warm, genuine — not transactional. Ask how she's doing. Mean it.

Small talk:
- Comment on the facility, the growth ("40 years in DFW — you've seen this market transform")
- Let Woody talk. He's a founder. Founders love to tell the origin story. Let him go 2-3 minutes.

**Your only job minutes 0–5:** Make Woody feel like he's in the room with someone trustworthy, not a financial buyer trying to strip his company.

---

### MINUTES 5–10: Frame the Conversation

Set the agenda out loud. Don't let the meeting drift.

> "Woody, here's what I'd like to do today. I want to show you the structure I've put together — the economics, how it works for you and your family. Then I want to show you what I plan to do with GFS after close. And then I just want to know if it's something worth exploring further. Sound good?"

**Key:** *"worth exploring further"* — low pressure framing. You're not asking him to sign anything.

Transition:
> "Before I get into the numbers, I want to say something. Benji told me how you built this company. 40 years. From nothing. That matters to me. I'm not here to buy an asset. I'm here to take something great and keep building it."

---

### MINUTES 10–20: The Value Stack

Walk him through the economics. Slowly. Let the numbers land.

**Lead with the headline:**
> "Woody, the structure I've put together delivers you $19 million in total value over 5 years. Here's how it breaks down."

Then break it down:

1. **$7.5M business acquisition price** — "That's what we're paying for the operating company."
2. **$2.5M for the real estate** — "You get full value for the property — $2.5 million."
3. **Seller note: $2M at 6%** — "That pays you $38,666 every month for 60 months — $2.32M total with interest. Predictable income, starting at close."
4. **18% equity rollover** — "You stay in the company. Your 18% rides with everything we build."

**Then make the comparison (only if he hesitates or mentions cash offer):**
> "I know what a cash sale looks like. At $14M, after capital gains, depreciation recapture, and fees — you're walking away with maybe $9.7 million. Our structure puts $12.3 million in your pocket in cash and contractual payments — $10 million at close, and a seller note that pays you $38,666 every month for 5 years. That's locked in. The 18% equity is on top of all of that."

**Let it sit.** Don't rush. If he's quiet, stay quiet. Let him process.

---

### MINUTES 20–23: The Growth Pitch (Plant the Seed)

Once he's nodding — transition to the vision. Keep this under 3 minutes. Don't open a laptop.

> "Woody, GFS is already a great business. 34% growth last year. $19M in revenue. Zero debt. You built something real. What I'm going to do is take what you built and turn it into something even bigger — and your 18% equity rides with every dollar of that."

**Move 1: Unlock the Hidden Revenue**
> "Right now, less than 10% of GFS revenue comes from inspections and monitoring. Every building you've ever put a system in is required by law to get inspected every year. That's your customers — and they're paying someone else right now. We're going to fix that. I'm targeting 25% of revenue from recurring service within 3 years. That alone re-rates what this company is worth."

**Move 2: Grow to $25M**
> "With a dedicated service team and focused sales, GFS gets to $25M organically. DFW is one of the fastest-growing markets in the country — data centers, hospitals, commercial builds. You're already in all of it. We just have to show up and sell the service side."

**Move 3: Platform Play**
> "There are dozens of small fire inspection shops across DFW — good operators, great customers, no succession plan. We acquire 1 or 2 of them, absorb the contract books, and we're at $40–50M combined. That's when this becomes a platform, not just a company."

**The Punchline:**
> "Here's what that means for you. The deal we're talking about already gets you $19 million. But your 18% doesn't stop at Year 5. If we hit $50M in revenue — and I intend to — this company is worth $50–70 million at exit. Your 18% of that is $9–12 million. So the question isn't whether $19M is a good deal. It is. But the real question is: do you want to own 18% of a $70 million company? Because that's what we're building."

---

### MINUTES 23–30: His Turn — Listen

> "Woody, I've done a lot of talking. What's your reaction? What questions do you have?"

**Then be quiet.**

His questions will tell you where he is:

| What he asks | What it means | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| "Who else is involved?" | He wants to vet your team | Name Danny + Mark. Frame them as his guys staying on. |
| "How does the note get paid?" | Wants security | "Business cash flow covers it with 2.3x room. It's not contingent on growth." |
| "What happens to my employees?" | Emotional concern | "They're the business. Danny and Mark stay. The team stays." |
| "What about my customers?" | Account relationship concern | "Your relationships don't go anywhere. Benji stays close. We don't disrupt what's working." |
| "I need to talk to my accountant/attorney" | Healthy, not a no | "Of course. I'll give you everything they need. What's the timeline?" |
| "I've had other offers" | Testing you | "I know. And I'd encourage you to compare them — specifically the after-tax math." |

---

### MINUTES 30–35: Handle the Objections

**If he raises a cash offer:**
> "Woody, let me show you something. At $14M cash, after capital gains at 20%, depreciation recapture at 25%, and fees — your take-home is roughly $9.7 million. Our structure delivers $12.3 million in cash and contractual payments — $10 million at close plus your seller note. That beats a cash sale before the equity even enters the picture. I'm not asking you to give up cash — I'm asking you to get more of it."

If he pushes harder on cash:
- Increase seller note to $3M ($58K/mo)
- Add $1M performance earnout at Year 3
- Bump equity to 20%
- Last resort: raise headline to $12M

**If he's concerned about losing control:**
> "Woody, you built this company for 40 years. I'm not coming in to change what works. I'm coming in to build what comes next. And you're still in it — with 18% equity and a seat at the table."

**If he brings up his health:**
Be human. Don't pivot to business.
> "Woody, I know this is a big decision. There's no pressure here. I'd rather take the time to get this right than rush anything."

---

### MINUTES 35–40: The Leave-Behind + Close

Hand him the one-pager. `gfs-woody-one-pager.md` — printed, clean, no branding.

> "Here's a one-pager that summarizes what we talked about. Take your time with it. Show it to your accountant, your attorney, Diane."

**The Closing Question:**
> "Woody, does this structure make sense to you? Is this something you'd want to keep exploring?"

**If yes:**
> "Great. Here's what happens next. I'll get you a formal letter of intent within [X days]. Nothing binding — just puts the structure on paper. Your team and my team can start talking about what due diligence looks like. Sound good?"

**If he wants time:**
> "Of course. What's a good timeline for you to think it through? I want to be respectful of your process."

**If no:**
> "I appreciate your time, Woody. Can I ask — is it the structure, the timing, or something else? I want to make sure I'm not missing something important."

---

### MINUTES 40–45: Exit Strong

Don't drag the meeting. Once the closing question is answered — wrap up gracefully.

- Thank him for his time
- Thank Diane if present
- Reference Benji warmly ("Benji speaks so highly of what you built here")
- Leave the one-pager
- Don't linger. Confident exit.

Follow up within 24 hours: handwritten note (or at minimum a direct text, not email).

If everything else falls apart, remember this:

**Woody's emotional anchor is legacy.** Not the money — the 40 years. The employees. The company name.

Speak to that, and everything else becomes easier.

> "You spent 40 years building something great. I'm going to spend the next 10 making sure it becomes something extraordinary. And you're going to get paid twice — once at the deal, and again at the exit."
